Act I

The Question

Before we build anything, let's get clear on what RL post-training infrastructure even is. The hardest part of this field is that the problem statement looks simple ("train an LLM with RL") but the systems requirements are nothing like training or inference alone. These three lessons set up the central tension that the rest of the course resolves.

Lesson 1

What does RL infrastructure actually solve?

A large language model is already trained. Why do we need a whole new kind of infrastructure to apply RL to it?

Pretraining an LLM is a static dataset + a training loop. Inference is a model + a request stream. Both are well-understood; you'd reach for Megatron-LM and vLLM respectively. But RL post-training is both at once: the model must generate fresh data (rollouts), those rollouts must be scored, and the policy must be updated from those scores — repeatedly, for millions of steps.

The problem this creates: the same model that's being trained must also serve inference for rollouts, on the same hardware, alternating in time. Whatever clever optimizations you applied to training (high tensor parallelism, optimizer offload) work against inference (where you want data parallelism for throughput). Whatever you applied to inference (KV cache, prefix sharing, CUDA graphs) costs memory the trainer wants back.

So RL post-training infrastructure is the discipline of making one cluster simultaneously good at training and inference, with the constraint that the policy keeps changing and the inference engine has to keep up. Every primitive in this course is a way of resolving this constraint cheaply enough to be economical at scale.

The shortest possible definition. RL infra = the engineering that lets a single GPU cluster alternate between "training mode" and "rollout mode" thousands of times per training run, while keeping the inference engine's weights perfectly in sync with the trainer's gradient updates.
Next: The two halves don't just need to coexist — they have opposing resource preferences. Lesson 2 — Two halves that fight.
Lesson 2

Two halves that fight

If training and inference both need GPUs, why can't we just give half to each?

Imagine you have 64 GPUs. You give 32 to training and 32 to inference. During rollout, the 32 training GPUs sit idle waiting for new data. During the training step, the 32 inference GPUs sit idle waiting for new weights. The on-policy constraint of RL forces these two phases to alternate, not run concurrently — which means half your fleet is always idle.

Worse, training and inference want different parallelism strategies:

ConcernTraining prefersRollout prefers
ParallelismHigh TP / PP — split the modelHigh DP — split the batch
MemoryOptimizer states + gradients + activationsWeights + KV cache + CUDA graphs
Throughput unitTokens/step (compute-bound)Trajectories/sec (memory-bound)
GoalUpdate one global policyGenerate N independent samples

If you force identical parallelization on both, one side bottlenecks. If you give them different parallelization, you need expensive parameter resharding every time you switch phases. Every elegant primitive that follows is a way out of this trap.

Next: So the alternative is to colocate them — but that has its own difficulties. Lesson 3 — Why this is harder than it looks.
Lesson 3

Why this is harder than it looks

Even if you colocate training and rollout on the same GPUs, three pain points remain. What are they?

Colocation solves the idle-fleet problem (training and inference now take turns owning the GPU), but it creates three new ones:

1. Memory choreography. When inference owns the GPU, it has weights + KV cache + CUDA graphs loaded. When training owns it, it needs optimizer + gradients + activations. They don't fit at once. You need a clean, race-free way to swap memory ownership. (Lesson 8 will show you the two functions that do this.)

2. Weight synchronization. After every training step, the trainer has new policy weights; the inference engine still has the old ones. You need a way to push the new weights into the inference engine — fast, lossless, and without restarting it. Naive "copy params from trainer to inference" approaches cost tens of milliseconds per parameter; for a 70B model that's minutes per training step. (Lessons 9–10 show the zero-copy trick.)

3. The numerical-precision trap. If the inference engine uses FP8 and the trainer uses BF16, the same input can produce slightly different logits, especially when MoE expert routing is involved. The training data becomes "lies about the current policy." The model trains on phantom signal. (Lesson 7 introduces the colocate pattern that exposes this issue; Lesson 12 connects it to AReaL/Miles staleness corrections.)

These three problems — memory, weight sync, numerical alignment — are why RL infra is a discipline of its own. The rest of the course is the engineered answers.

Act II

The Universal Pattern

Every framework — Miles, SLIME, verl, SkyRL, RLinf, cosmos-rl, AReaL, OpenRLHF, even SGLang's RL adopters — instantiates the same skeleton: a five-stage cycle around three pillars. Once you see this shape, you can read any of their train.py files in 15 minutes. These three lessons are the most important in the course.

Lesson 4

The rollout cycle

What is "one step" of RL training, drawn as a flowchart?

One step of RL post-training is a five-stage loop. The model generates completions, those are scored by a reward function, low-quality ones are filtered out, the survivors update the policy, and the new policy is pushed back to the inference engine. Then it starts over.

Figure 1 — The cycle every RL framework instantiates. Latency of one full loop dominates RL economics.

Every primitive in this course exists to make one stage of this cycle cheaper. RadixAttention makes stage ① faster. Reward microservices make ② non-blocking. The four update_weights_from_* paths make ⑤ zero-copy. Once you can locate this loop in any repo's train.py, you understand 80% of the framework.

Mental model. A framework is "the set of choices it makes about how to implement each of these five stages, plus how to swap memory between them." Differences between Miles and SLIME, between verl and OpenRLHF, all map back to choices in this cycle.
Next: The cycle runs on three pillars. Lesson 5 — The three pillars.
Lesson 5

The three pillars

What are the three components every RL framework stitches together?

Every framework is built around three components: a training engine that computes gradients and holds the optimizer; an inference engine that runs the policy to generate rollouts; and an orchestrator that coordinates them across GPUs and nodes. Around them sits a shared data layer (reward hub, filter hub, data buffer) and an algorithm layer (GRPO, PPO, DAPO, OPD).

Figure 2 — Picking a row from each column gives you a framework. (Megatron + SGLang + Ray) = Miles, SLIME, verl, RLinf.

The genius of the modern RL stack is that these three pillars are pluggable. You can swap Megatron for FSDP, SGLang for vLLM, Ray for custom NCCL — and the framework still works because each pillar exposes a stable interface. We'll see those interfaces concretely in Lessons 9-11.

Act III

The Engineered Solutions

Six lessons, six primitives. Each one resolves one piece of the central tension we set up in Acts I-II. By the end of this act you will know every non-trivial design choice in modern RL post-training infrastructure. These are the lessons that took the field years to figure out.

Lesson 7

The hybrid engine — 训推一体

Colocate training and inference on the same GPUs and serialize them in time. Why does this win over disaggregation?

The answer to "two halves that fight" (Lesson 2) is a pattern called 训推一体 ("integrated train-inference"), first formalized in verl's HybridFlow paper. The idea is simple: put both halves on the same GPUs and run them serially, swapping memory between phases.

ChoiceCost of the alternative
Why colocateHybrid engine puts train + rollout on the same GPUs, runs them seriallyDisaggregate: rollout GPUs idle during training, training GPUs idle during rollout. With on-policy constraint forcing serialization, half your fleet is always idle.
Engineering benefit85-90% GPU utilization (vs 45-55% disaggregate)Disaggregate also doubles inter-cluster bandwidth for weight sync.
Scaling propertyWorks up to ~64 GPUs as full colocate; above that, split colocate (actor+ref on one pool, critic+reward on another) winsPure disaggregate becomes feasible only above ~1024 GPUs when rollout volume can amortize separate inference clusters.

Every modern framework — Miles, SLIME, verl, SkyRL, RLinf, cosmos-rl — uses some form of this pattern. The disagreement is on how to swap memory, which is Lesson 8.

Next: The two functions that swap memory and make it work. Lesson 8 — Memory choreography.
Lesson 8

Memory choreography

If inference and training share the GPU, how do you safely hand memory between them without races?

The hybrid engine works because two functions — release_memory_occupation and resume_memory_occupation — encode the entire concurrency contract. Here is the timeline of one cycle:

Figure 3 — One GPU, two roles. The "owner" lane shows who's running; the "memory" lane shows what's loaded.

And here is the real implementation, from SGLang's mixin:

python/sglang/srt/managers/scheduler_update_weights_mixin.py· tag-based memory pause 
def release_memory_occupation(self, recv_req):
    assert self.is_fully_idle(), \
        "release_memory_occupation should be called only when server is idle."

    tags = recv_req.tags or GPU_MEMORY_ALL_TYPES
    for tag in tags:
        self.offload_tags.add(tag)

    if GPU_MEMORY_TYPE_KV_CACHE in tags:
        self.memory_saver_adapter.pause(GPU_MEMORY_TYPE_KV_CACHE)
        self.flush_cache()

    if GPU_MEMORY_TYPE_WEIGHTS in tags:
        self.stashed_model_static_state = _export_static_state(
            self.tp_worker.model_runner.model
        )
        torch.distributed.barrier(self.tp_cpu_group)
        self.memory_saver_adapter.pause(GPU_MEMORY_TYPE_WEIGHTS)

    if GPU_MEMORY_TYPE_CUDA_GRAPH in tags:
        self.memory_saver_adapter.pause(GPU_MEMORY_TYPE_CUDA_GRAPH)

    torch.get_device_module().synchronize()
    return ReleaseMemoryOccupationReqOutput()
What makes this elegant. The assert is_fully_idle() at the top is the concurrency contract — the function literally cannot run if any request is in flight. The three independent if tag in tags blocks mean colocated training can free just KV cache (keep weights warm for fast resume) or just weights (the trainer needs the memory). Granularity is the whole point.
Next: Memory is now safely swappable. But after the trainer updates weights, how do we get them back into the inference engine? Lesson 9 — The weight-sync handoff.
Lesson 9

The weight-sync handoff

The trainer just produced θt+1. How does it move into the inference engine without restarting it — and without copying 70GB of tensors?

Naive weight sync: serialize the new parameters to a buffer, send the buffer to the inference process, deserialize, replace. On a 70B model this is 50+ ms per parameter, with thousands of parameters per layer. Result: minutes per training step. Untenable.

The trick is to never move the tensor data at all. The trainer process has the new weights in GPU memory. The inference process is on the same host (or shares a NCCL group). Instead of copying bytes, the trainer sends a handle tuple — a few hundred bytes of pointer metadata, stride, offset, and CUDA IPC descriptor. The inference process reconstructs a Python tensor that points to the same physical GPU memory.

Compared to a 50ms-per-param copy, handle-tuple sync is sub-millisecond. This single trick is what makes colocated weight sync economical. Step 13 in Lesson 6 is the line that does it.

The pitfall. verl's implementation requires monkey_patch_torch_reductions() to register CUDA IPC handle deserializers. Missing this call causes silent segfaults on the SGLang side, but only intermittently. This is the kind of bug that costs a week.
Next: Handle tuples work for same-process trainers. What about checkpointing to disk, NCCL across nodes, or CUDA IPC across processes? Lesson 10 — Four update_weights paths.
Lesson 10

Four update_weights_from_* paths, one verb

Different deployment topologies need different transports. How does SGLang present one interface that handles all of them?

SGLang exposes four implementations behind one verb. The framework above writes engine.update_weights_from_*(...) — SGLang internally handles tensor reconstruction, dtype preservation under quantization, and the lock that prevents concurrent generation.

Figure 4 — One verb, four transports. Each maps to a real deployment topology.

And here is the actual mixin code — notice that all four methods share the same shape and the same flush_cache_after_weight_update call (the RadixCache must be invalidated because old prefix entries reference the old policy):

python/sglang/srt/managers/scheduler_update_weights_mixin.py· four transports, one verb 
class SchedulerUpdateWeightsMixin:
    def update_weights_from_disk(self, recv_req):
        """In-place update of the weights from disk."""
        success, message = self.tp_worker.update_weights_from_disk(recv_req)
        if success and self.draft_worker is not None:
            success, message = self.draft_worker.update_weights_from_disk(recv_req)
        if success:
            self.flush_cache_after_weight_update(recv_req)
        return UpdateWeightFromDiskReqOutput(success, message, 0)

    def update_weights_from_distributed(self, recv_req):
        """Update the online model parameter."""
        success, message = self.tp_worker.update_weights_from_distributed(recv_req)
        if success:
            self.flush_cache_after_weight_update(recv_req)
        return UpdateWeightsFromDistributedReqOutput(success, message)

    def update_weights_from_tensor(self, recv_req):
        """Update the online model parameter from tensors."""
        worker = self.draft_worker or self.tp_worker
        success, message = worker.update_weights_from_tensor(recv_req)
        if success:
            self.flush_cache_after_weight_update(recv_req)
        torch.distributed.barrier(group=self.tp_cpu_group)
        return UpdateWeightsFromTensorReqOutput(success, message)

    def update_weights_from_ipc(self, recv_req):
        """Update the online model parameter from IPC for checkpoint-engine integration."""
        success, message = self.tp_worker.update_weights_from_ipc(recv_req)
        if success and self.draft_worker is not None:
            success, message = self.draft_worker.update_weights_from_ipc(recv_req)
        if success:
            self.flush_cache_after_weight_update(recv_req)
        torch.distributed.barrier(group=self.tp_cpu_group)
        return UpdateWeightsFromIPCReqOutput(success, message)
An underrated constraint. NCCL process groups fix participants at creation. Adding a new inference node mid-training means destroying and recreating the group. RDMA's point-to-point model treats each connection independently. This single fact explains why colocated frameworks (Miles, SLIME) lean on NCCL via init_weights_update_group, while disaggregated systems (cosmos-rl, SkyRL's remote inference) prefer RDMA or disk for weight sync. Elasticity is incompatible with NCCL.
Next: Weight sync is solved. What about generation itself — is there an algorithm-system co-design that makes rollouts faster? Lesson 11 — RadixAttention × GRPO.
Lesson 11

RadixAttention × GRPO — multiplicative composition

GRPO generates N completions per prompt. They share an identical prefix. Can the inference engine exploit that?

GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) is the dominant RL algorithm in 2025-2026. It generates a group of N completions per prompt and normalizes rewards within the group as a baseline. The system-level consequence is that all N completions share the prompt prefix — and RadixAttention turns that into a near-free win.

Figure 5 — Without prefix cache: 4× prefill cost, 4× KV memory. With RadixAttention: 1× prefill, 1× shared KV, near-free decode.

The radix tree organizes shared prefixes as ancestor nodes; each completion branches off as a child. Reference counting (inc_lock_ref / dec_lock_ref) means a prefix can't be evicted while any completion still uses it. A node with lock_ref > 0 simply cannot be evicted — use-after-free is unrepresentable.

python/sglang/srt/mem_cache/radix_cache.py· the six methods of the prefix tree 
class RadixCache(BasePrefixCache, KVCacheEventMixin):
    def match_prefix(self, params: MatchPrefixParams) -> MatchResult:
        """Walk the tree, return the longest matching prefix's KV slots."""

    def insert(self, params: InsertParams) -> InsertResult:
        """Add a new completion's tail tokens as descendant nodes."""

    def inc_lock_ref(self, node: TreeNode) -> IncLockRefResult:
        """Pin a node — its KV slots cannot be evicted while lock_ref > 0."""

    def dec_lock_ref(self, ...) -> DecLockRefResult:
        """Release pin — node becomes evictable when no requests reference it."""

    def evict(self, params: EvictParams) -> EvictResult:
        """LRU/LFU/SLRU eviction of evictable nodes only — pinned nodes survive."""

    @property
    def evictable_size(self):
        """How much memory can we reclaim if we evict everything unpinned?"""
Why this is elegant. Neither was designed for the other. GRPO chose group sampling for variance reduction. RadixAttention chose a tree for cache reuse. Their composition multiplies savings — and reference counting means cache lifetime is automatic.
Next: One last source of latency: waiting for the longest rollout to finish before training starts. Lesson 12 — Async & staleness.
Lesson 12

Async training and staleness corrections

If 10% of rollouts take 20 seconds and 90% finish in 2 seconds, do we wait for the slow ones?

Strict on-policy RL says: wait for every trajectory to finish under policy θt before running the gradient step. In practice this is fatal. If 10% of prompts are long-context (2K tokens decode) and 90% are short (512 tokens), the short ones finish in ~2s but the long ones take ~20s. 90% of GPUs idle for 18s every iteration. Over 1000 iterations that's 5 GPU-hours per GPU wasted.

Partial rollout: train on the 128 trajectories that finished, let the rest continue under (now-stale) θt. Throughput 2-4× higher; off-policy ratio rises but it can be controlled in two ways:

  • Mathematically — Miles applies TIS (Truncated Importance Sampling) and MIS (Masked Importance Sampling) corrections that re-weight stale samples to recover an unbiased estimator.
  • Operationally — Kimi K1.5's approach: context-length checkpointing, dynamic batch reordering, and prefix-maximizing strategies bound the staleness without explicit IS weights.

The architectural extreme is AReaL's fully-async design: NCCL broadcasts are launched with async_op=True and bucketed by memory budget. The trainer never blocks on weight sync — it queues bucket after bucket and continues. Compared to OpenRLHF's synchronous broadcast_to_vllm() (which blocks until all engines acknowledge), AReaL claims 2.77× speedup.

The principle. The system tells you it's lying about being on-policy. The IS weights are explicit; the staleness is a parameter, not a bug. This is "rational compromise between engineering efficiency and algorithmic rigor" — but the compromise is visible in the code, not hidden behind it.

Lesson 13

verl's fit() loop — the canonical PPO orchestration

What does the heart of a real RL framework look like in code?

verl's ray_trainer.py is the cleanest mapping of "RL algorithm → orchestration code" in the open-source ecosystem. Every phase of PPO is a marked_timer block; every cross-module call is a Ray dispatch; every batch update is a DataProto.union(...). The driver code is <30 lines for an entire step.

verl/trainer/ppo/ray_trainer.py· main training step · L1370+ 
with marked_timer("step", timing_raw):
    with marked_timer("gen", timing_raw, color="red"):
        combined_gen_output = self.async_rollout_manager.generate_sequences(combined_gen_batch)
        self.checkpoint_manager.sleep_replicas()

    batch = batch.repeat(repeat_times=self.config.actor_rollout_ref.rollout.n, interleave=True)
    batch = batch.union(gen_batch_output)

    with marked_timer("reward", timing_raw, color="yellow"):
        if self.use_rm and "rm_scores" not in batch.batch.keys():
            batch_reward = self._compute_reward_colocate(batch)
            batch = batch.union(batch_reward)
        reward_tensor, reward_extra_infos_dict = extract_reward(batch)

    with marked_timer("old_log_prob", timing_raw, color="blue"):
        old_log_prob, old_log_prob_mfu = self._compute_old_log_prob(batch)
        batch = batch.union(old_log_prob)

    if self.use_reference_policy:
        with marked_timer(str(Role.RefPolicy), timing_raw, color="olive"):
            ref_log_prob = self._compute_ref_log_prob(batch)
            batch = batch.union(ref_log_prob)

    if self.use_critic:
        with marked_timer("values", timing_raw, color="cyan"):
            values = self._compute_values(batch)
            batch = batch.union(values)

    with marked_timer("adv", timing_raw, color="brown"):
        batch = compute_advantage(
            batch, adv_estimator=self.config.algorithm.adv_estimator,
            gamma=self.config.algorithm.gamma, lam=self.config.algorithm.lam,
            num_repeat=self.config.actor_rollout_ref.rollout.n,
            norm_adv_by_std_in_grpo=norm_adv_by_std_in_grpo, config=self.config.algorithm,
        )

    if self.use_critic:
        with marked_timer("update_critic", timing_raw, color="pink"):
            critic_output = self._update_critic(batch)

    with marked_timer("update_actor", timing_raw, color="red"):
        actor_output = self._update_actor(batch)
What this teaches. The colors are not decorative — they correspond to phases you'd see in verl's wandb traces. Red = generation, yellow = reward, blue = old_log_prob, etc. The driver code is short because every method call is a Ray dispatch to SPMD worker groups. Reading this once is worth more than reading 20 papers.
Next: What is the batch: DataProto that flows through this loop, and how does the dispatch work? Lesson 14 — DataProto + dispatch.
Lesson 14

The DataProto envelope and SPMD dispatch

What is the data structure that flows between modules, and how does "single line on the driver" become "100 GPUs running in parallel"?

Every batch that flows between modules in verl is a DataProto. Three fields: batch (TensorDict for tensors), non_tensor_batch (numpy arrays for things like images), meta_info (Python dict for metadata). Polymorphic indexing supports int, slice, list, numpy, and torch tensor.

verl/protocol.py· the universal data envelope 
@dataclass
class DataProto:
    """A DataProto is a data structure for data exchange between functions."""
    batch: TensorDict = None
    non_tensor_batch: dict = field(default_factory=dict)
    meta_info: dict = field(default_factory=dict)

    def __getitem__(self, item):
        """Enhanced indexing supporting int, slice, list, numpy array, torch tensor."""
        if isinstance(item, slice):
            return self.slice(item.start, item.stop, item.step)
        elif isinstance(item, list | np.ndarray | torch.Tensor):
            return self.select_idxs(item)
        elif isinstance(item, int | np.integer):
            return self._get_item(item)

And the SPMD dispatch is where "one driver call" expands into "100 GPUs running":

verl/single_controller/base/decorator.py· dispatch / collect for SPMD work groups 
def dispatch_dp_compute_data_proto(worker_group, *args, **kwargs):
    """Splits DataProto args across worker_group ranks with auto-padding."""
    assert isinstance(worker_group, WorkerGroup)
    splitted_args, splitted_kwargs = _split_args_kwargs_data_proto_with_auto_padding(
        worker_group.world_size, *args, **kwargs,
    )
    return splitted_args, splitted_kwargs

def collect_dp_compute_data_proto(worker_group, output):
    """Gather results from all workers and concatenate DataProto outputs."""
    assert BatchData(output).is_concatable()
    output = collect_dp_compute(worker_group, output)
    return _concat_data_proto_or_future(output)
What this teaches. "Single-controller interface, multi-controller execution." The framework looks single-threaded; it runs SPMD across hundreds of GPUs. Auto-padding handles the case where batch size isn't divisible by world_size. This pattern (dispatch / execute / collect) is the universal RL workflow primitive — RLinf's M2Flow generalizes it.

Lesson 15

AReaL vs OpenRLHF — async in one keyword

What's the single keyword that distinguishes "synchronous" from "fully async" RL?

AReaL's fully-async design and OpenRLHF's synchronous design differ in one keyword: async_op=True. AReaL launches NCCL broadcasts that return immediately; OpenRLHF blocks until every engine acknowledges.

AReaL: launch and continue

    futures: list[torch.cuda.Event]
    tensors: list[torch.Tensor]

# During weight sync:
for bucket in buckets:
    dist.broadcast(bucket.tensor, src=0, group=dp_group, async_op=True)

# Inference side:

    meta, param_specs, async_op=True

OpenRLHF: synchronous broadcast

@ray.remote(num_gpus=1)
class PolicyModelActor(BaseModelActor):
    def fit(self, kl_ctl):
        return self.trainer.ppo_train(kl_ctl)

    def broadcast_to_vllm(self, vllm_engines):
        # synchronous: waits for all engines to acknowledge
        for engine in vllm_engines:
            engine.update_weights.remote(...)
        # blocks here until done
What this teaches. The structural limit Chenyang Zhao identified in his tutorial. OpenRLHF's design is correct but synchronous. Each step pays a fixed weight-sync latency before the next rollout can begin. At 256 GPUs with frequent updates, that latency cost grows from a nuisance to the dominant term in step time. AReaL and verl's async paths exist because of this limit.

Next: Quick tour of each framework's distinctive contribution. Lesson 19 — Tour of the 9.
Lesson 19

Tour of the 9 frameworks

In one paragraph each: what's the framework about, and what file should you read first?

verl — the hybrid-engine reference implementation

verl (Volcano Engine RL, originally ByteDance Seed) is the open-source version of the HybridFlow paper. Most patterns we've discussed — handle-tuple weight sync, single-controller dispatch, ResourcePoolManager, marked_timer phases — were canonicalized here. Trained Doubao-1.5-pro, Seed-Thinking-v1.5, DAPO-32B. Read first: verl/trainer/ppo/ray_trainer.py.

Miles — bit-identical RL for frontier MoEs

Miles targets the hardest case: training trillion-parameter MoE models where any numerical drift between inference and training collapses learning. Its signature contributions — Unified FP8 Pipeline, R3 routing replay, INT4 quant-aware training — all attack this single problem. Read first: miles/backends/ for FP8 configs.

SLIME — RL flexibility for reasoning + agents

SLIME trains GLM-5.1 and emphasizes algorithmic flexibility over numerical sophistication. Signature contribution: on-policy distillation. Read first: slime/rollout/ — particularly on_policy_distillation.py.

SkyRL — modular RL for agents

SkyRL (Berkeley NovaSky) bets on modularity. Five sub-packages: skyrl-train, skyrl-gym, skyrl-agent, skyrl-tx (Tinker), plus unified skyrl/. Trained SA-SWE-32B on SWEBench. Read first: skyrl-gym/.

cosmos-rl — RL for physical AI (NVIDIA)

cosmos-rl is the only framework that isn't chat-LLM-centric. "Flexible and scalable RL framework specialized for Physical AI." Diffusion world models, VLA robotics, video generation. Custom controller-replica with dynamic NCCL — no Ray. Read first: cosmos_rl/policy/.

RLinf — flexibility through scheduling

RLinf ("Reinforcement Learning Infrastructure" / "Infinite") argues that the core problem in RL infra is system flexibility. Their answer: M2Flow. Up to 2.43× throughput from scheduling alone. Three arxiv papers (2509.15965, 2510.06710, 2602.07837). Read first: rlinf/scheduler/.

OpenRLHF — the OG, structurally limited

OpenRLHF was the first widely-adopted open-source RLHF framework. Synchronous design limits scaling, but it's the easiest framework to read end-to-end. Every modern framework's architecture is a response to OpenRLHF's structural choices. Read first: openrlhf/trainer/ppo_trainer.py.

AReaL — fully-async RL

AReaL (Inclusion AI) makes fully asynchronous first-class. _PendingWeightUpdateBucket queues NCCL broadcasts; trainer never blocks. 2.77× speedup over synchronous designs. Read first: areal/engine/fsdp_engine.py.

SGLang — the substrate

SGLang's README explicitly calls itself "a proven rollout backend used for training many frontier models, with native RL integrations." Adopted by Miles, SLIME, AReaL, verl, Tunix. The picks-and-shovels everyone else builds on. Read first: scheduler_update_weights_mixin.py — the four update_weights_from_* verbs.

Act VI

Beyond Chat

So far we've assumed the target is a chat LLM. But the 2026 frontier is wider — multi-turn agents, vision-language-action policies, diffusion world models, video generators. These targets push different requirements onto the framework, and frameworks that can't accommodate them get left behind. Three lessons on what changes when you leave the chat domain.

Lesson 20

Multi-turn agents and tool use

What changes when a "rollout" is a 10-turn conversation with API calls, not a single completion?

Single-turn RL: one prompt → one completion → one reward. Easy. Multi-turn RL: the model emits a tool call, gets a tool response, emits another action, gets another response — across 10+ turns. Reward arrives at the end (task success) or per-turn (intermediate signals). The rollout is now a trajectory, not a completion.

The systems requirements change:

  • KV cache reuse across turns — SGLang's open_session() / close_session(). Without it, every turn re-prefills the entire history.
  • Variable-length trajectories — some agents finish in 2 turns, some in 20. Long-tail is brutal. Partial rollout (Lesson 12) becomes mandatory.
  • Environment as a service — the agent needs to call real tools (a search API, a code executor, a database). The framework needs a clean environment abstraction.

Two frameworks lead here: SkyRL with its skyrl-gym + skyrl-agent sub-packages (trained the SA-SWE-32B model on SWEBench), and SLIME with concrete examples (tau-bench, retool, search-r1). verl's examples/sglang_multiturn shows the ReTool recipe.

Next: If multi-turn agents are one extension, embodied AI is the other — and it has even harder requirements. Lesson 21 — Embodied AI, VLA, world models.
Lesson 21

Embodied AI, VLA, and world models

What does an RL framework need when the target outputs robot actions or video frames instead of tokens?

Three new model classes have appeared:

  • Vision-Language-Action (VLA) — π₀ / π₀.₅ (Physical Intelligence), OpenVLA, NVIDIA GR00T-N1.5. Take image + language instruction; output continuous action sequences for robots.
  • World foundation models (WFM) — Cosmos-Predict 2B/14B, SANA, SD3. Diffusion-based video generators that can simulate environments.
  • Diffusion image models — FLUX.1 (schnell, dev, Canny, Depth, Redux), FLUX.2 (klein for sub-second inference, dev for quality). Targets for RL refinement via reward models (pickscore, aesthetic).

The systems requirements:

  • Diffusion is iterative — each "rollout" is 50+ denoising network calls, not one autoregressive pass. Cost-per-sample dominates. Batching at the trajectory level (not the token level) is the only economic path.
  • Wan2.2 is MoE video — 27B total params, ~14B active per timestep. Multi-GPU inference via FSDP and DeepSpeed Ulysses.
  • Long context — video tokens reach 100K+. cosmos-rl's 6D parallelism (Sequence × Tensor × Context × Pipeline × FSDP × DDP) exists to handle this.
  • DDRL (Data-regularized DRL) — cosmos-rl's diffusion-model RL algorithm; replaces KL with reward + standard diffusion training.

Two frameworks cover this terrain. cosmos-rl is the only one with a diffusers backend and DDRL. RLinf (as of Feb 2026) supports RL fine-tuning of VLA on Wan world models — closing the loop where Wan simulates rollouts for embodied agents.

Next: One more environment frontier — what if you're not on GPUs at all? Lesson 22 — The TPU detour.
Lesson 22

The TPU detour

If you're forced onto Google TPUs, can you still use any of this?

The Cambrian-MLLM TPU training blog documents two years of training multimodal models on TPUs. The systems-level findings are directly relevant to RL infra:

  • "Dynamic shapes are the enemy" — every shape change triggers XLA recompilation (minutes). RL pipelines on TPU must use fixed batch sizes and dummy-image padding. Variable-length generation as we know it on GPU is essentially banned.
  • "Arbitrary SPMD sharding proved impractical" — the team hit three critical bugs and fell back to "FSDP via SPMD." Ray-based hybrid placement doesn't translate.
  • Silent library incompatibilities — F.scaled_dot_product_attention and torch.utils.checkpoint fail without errors on TorchXLA.

The conclusion: a TPU-native RL framework would look fundamentally different. Static graph compilation, fixed shape contracts, no Ray, no SGLang. No one has built this yet. vLLM has plugin support for TPUs in inference, but the full RL-on-TPU story is open. This is a real research gap.

Next: The pitfalls that took the community years to figure out. Lesson 24 — Pitfalls (踩坑录).
Lesson 24

Pitfalls — lessons paid in pain (踩坑录)

What does Chenyang Zhao's tutorial call out as having burned the community? What were the painful lessons?

1. Training-inference numerical drift ("BERT-era unsolved bug")

Inference kernels fuse operations to maximize throughput; the fusion depends on batch shape. The same model + same input + different batch size = slightly different output. For sampled tokens this is invisible. For logprobs that drive PPO loss, accumulated drift makes the policy appear to update toward a target that doesn't exist.

Lesson: Never trust inference-engine logprobs for loss computation. Always recompute with the training engine.

2. Handle-tuple deserialization segfaults

verl's update_weights_from_tensor requires monkey_patch_torch_reductions() to register CUDA IPC handle deserializers. Missing this call → silent segfault on the SGLang side, but only intermittently.

Lesson: Handle tuples are low-level. The abstraction leaks. Read the integration code line by line.

3. NCCL hang under mixed inference backends

OpenRLHF + SGLang integration: "the entire process was very painful... there's still an NCCL hang error." NCCL hangs are silent deadlocks — no error, just a stalled run.

Lesson: Mixing distributed backends (DeepSpeed + Ray + SGLang's own dist) creates fragile NCCL group management.

4. Memory choreography under colocate

Megatron's CPU offload is "not perfect" — KV cache and model parameters contend for the same address ranges. slime's bucket-based weight update exists specifically to avoid OOM on large MoE models.

Lesson: The "memory hand-off" between train and rollout phases is the most common production failure mode. Test at full scale early.

5. Off-policy ratio drift

Partial rollout introduces staleness. Without correction (TIS/MIS) or operational bounds (Kimi's curriculum), the off-policy ratio grows monotonically until the policy is training on data from a fundamentally different policy. The training curve looks fine. The eval curve mysteriously degrades.

Lesson: Monitor off-policy ratio as a first-class metric. Cap staleness even if it costs throughput.

6. Mixing precisions across train and infer

BF16 training + FP8 inference + FP32 optimizer state = three numerical regimes interacting via weight sync. MoE expert routing diverges between regimes (the problem Miles' R3 was built to solve).

Lesson: If you can't make precision uniform, instrument routing decisions and gradients per-expert. The bug is invisible at the loss level.

The painful lesson behind all of these. Chenyang's most-repeated line, paraphrased: "A large number of RL conclusions derived from papers are based on RL infrastructure that may be extremely flawed." Every pitfall above produces results that look fine on the loss curve and fail silently on the policy. Infrastructure correctness precedes algorithmic speed.
